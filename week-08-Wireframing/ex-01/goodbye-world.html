Goodbye World!

Despite his early influence on hypertext theory, Nelson struggled to bring Project Xanadu to completion. Throughout the 1980s and 1990s, new technologies emerged that overshadowed his vision, most notably Tim Berners-Lee’s invention of the World Wide Web in 1989. The web achieved global adoption because it was simpler and easier to implement, even though Nelson criticised it as a weak imitation of his more ambitious ideas.

Nelson continued to refine his work, publishing Literary Machines (1981) and advocating for “deep links” and “transclusion” — concepts that still influence digital media and web design. While Project Xanadu never reached mainstream use, Nelson’s ideas remain part of the conversation about how we might design fairer, more interconnected information systems. His legacy lies not in a finished system, but in inspiring generations of thinkers, artists, and technologists to imagine alternatives to the web as we know it.
